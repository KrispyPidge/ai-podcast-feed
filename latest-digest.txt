Good morning! Here's your daily A.I. digest for Monday, April thirteen, two thousand and twenty-six.

Starting with the top story.

Anthropic has overtaken OpenAI in revenue for the first time, and the numbers are striking. Anthropic is now sitting at thirty billion dollars in annualised revenue, while OpenAI is at approximately twenty-five billion. That's a reversal nobody was predicting eighteen months ago. On top of that, Anthropic has doubled its enterprise client base and locked in a three-point-five gigawatt compute deal with Google and Broadcom, which is a massive infrastructure commitment. So the company that many people thought was just a safety-focused underdog is now leading the commercial A.I. race.

Tied to this story, OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google announced they're sharing intelligence through the Frontier Model Forum to fight what's called adversarial distillation, which is when companies use thousands of fake accounts to extract knowledge from a competitor's model and use it to train their own. Three Chinese A.I. companies were named directly: DeepSeek, Moonshot A.I., and MiniMax. Anthropic says these firms generated over sixteen million Claude exchanges through roughly twenty-four, thousand fraudulent accounts. It's a rare moment of cooperation between competitors, and it tells you a lot about where geopolitical tensions in A.I. are heading.

Next up, Ell Ell Ems and coding tools.

OpenAI launched G.P.T. five point four, which ships with a one-million-token context window and scored seventy-five percent on a benchmark called OSWorld-V, which tests how well an A.I. can perform real desktop productivity tasks autonomously. Think: the kind of multi-step work you'd normally hand to a junior assistant. OpenAI also launched a hundred-dollar-a-month Pro coding tier with five times more Codex usage than the Plus plan, aimed at developers doing heavy agentic coding work. That launch was partly a response to users being frustrated with Claude's usage limits, so there's clearly a battle forming around who owns the developer workflow.

On the open-weight side, Google dropped Gemma four, under the Apache two-point-oh licence, with a built-in sandboxed code execution tool that lets the model write, run, and test code mid-conversation. And Meta formally unveiled Muse Spark, the model family previously known as Llama. The headline model is Llama four, Maverick, with four, hundred billion parameters, a ten-million-token context window, and a new Contemplating reasoning mode that apparently benchmarks competitively against Claude Opus four, point six and Grok four, point twenty. The open-weight race is as fierce as it's ever been.

Also in this space: vibe coding, which is the practice of building software mostly through prompts rather than writing code line by line, has officially gone mainstream. Seventy-two percent of developers now use A.I. coding tools every day, and forty-one percent of all global code is A.I.-generated. Cursor shipped a new parallel agent interface this week, and OpenAI published an official plugin for Claude Code, meaning the three big coding stacks are starting to work together. Claude Code leads developer satisfaction with a forty-six percent most-loved rating, compared to Cursor at nineteen percent and GitHub Copilot at nine percent. And Anthropic's Model Context Protocol crossed ninety-seven million installs in March, which effectively makes it the default standard for connecting A.I. agents to real-world tools and data.

In industry and business news.

Perplexity has turned its Computer agent into a full personal finance hub, rolling out a Plaid integration so users can connect their bank accounts, credit cards, and loans directly to the agent. It can then build budgets, net worth trackers, debt payoff plans, and more, all from a simple text prompt. This comes on top of a U.S. tax filing integration they launched earlier this year. So Perplexity isn't just trying to replace Google anymore, it's going after Mint, TurboTax, and every financial app on your phone. Their annualised revenue hit four, hundred and fifty million dollars in March, which was a fifty percent jump in a single month.

Amazon's C.E.O. Andy Jassy used his annual shareholder letter to reveal A.I. revenue figures for the first time. A.W.S.'s A.I. division crossed fifteen billion dollars in annualised revenue, which is two hundred and sixty times where A.W.S. itself was at the same stage of its growth. Amazon's custom chips, the Trainium, Graviton, and Nitro lines, crossed twenty billion dollars in yearly revenue, and Jassy hinted they may start selling chip racks to outside buyers. Two unnamed customers apparently tried to purchase Amazon's entire 2026 Graviton supply. If you only look at model benchmarks, Amazon looks behind. But these numbers tell a very different story about who's actually winning in compute.

On the regulation front, the E.U. has now issued over seven billion dollars in fines against Google, Apple, and Meta since 2024, which is causing a real diplomatic clash with the U.S. government. Stateside, the White House published its National A.I. Policy Framework in March, recommending that federal law preempt state A.I. regulations and tying forty-two billion dollars in broadband funding to compliance. Whether you think that's good or bad policy, it shows that A.I. governance is moving from theoretical debates to actual enforcement with real financial consequences.

In gaming and A.I.

A G.D.C. survey published this week found that fifty-two percent of game developers now believe generative A.I. is hurting the industry, which is a sharp jump from previous years. Only seven percent see it as a net positive. The concerns are around job displacement, asset quality, and a feeling that A.I. is flattening the artistry of game development. If you're managing a gaming community or streaming games, this is worth paying attention to, because your audience is almost certainly having these conversations in chats, on Discord, and in forums. It's becoming a mainstream player sentiment, not just an industry insider debate.

On the technical side though, the A.I. in games is genuinely getting impressive. Unreal Engine six and Unity 2026 now have neural asset generation pipelines built in, so artists can describe an environment in words and get a fully textured three-D result in seconds. A.I. N.P.C. systems are moving beyond behaviour trees into persistent memory, meaning a character you offend early in a game might actually remember that interaction and respond differently later, not because a developer coded a flag, but because the N.P.C. has a generated sense of context. And A.I. voice synthesis means N.P.C.s can now deliver unscripted dialogue that sounds genuinely emotional. For streamers covering open-world titles, these kinds of emergent moments are going to become regular content as players start noticing and sharing them.
